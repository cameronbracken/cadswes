Basic Guidelines

á       The percent exceedance ranges outlined in Table 1 are defined in the 2000 Flow and Temperature Recommendations as the probability of occurrence of a given annual spring-runoff volume as determined from the historic record of inflow-runoff volumes to Flaming Gorge Dam. In practice this is from 1963 through the last year on record.  This year the hydrologic classifications will be based on a historic record from 1963-2009.  Hydrologic classifications apply to both spring and base flow operations.

Base Flow Operations (August-February)

á       August hydrologic classification based on April through July unregulated inflow forecast in Flaming Gorge Reservoir

á       Part of the adaptive management process is to set an average daily release associated with the appropriate hydrologic classification.  The average daily base flow can fluctuate ±40% around the average daily release during August-November, and ±25% during the December-February time period.  The ROD limits daily change to 3% rate of change per day, which has been implemented as 50 cfs/day for ease of operations.  This has been approved as standard operating practice by both the USFWS and WAPA.

January Considerations

á       Beginning March 1 the base flow restrictions end that limit flows to ±25% of the average daily base flow (set at 1,700 cfs at the beginning of the base flow period for 2009).  March through May 23 is a transition period to accumulate as much storage as possible and meet the May 1 ULDE. 

á       This month consideration was given to hydropower because of Glen Canyon Dam operations. February decreased and the March – May 23 period remained slightly higher in an attempt to conservatively operate for hydropower.

á       Spring 2010 flows are assumed to be a duration of 2 weeks at power plant capacity (4,300 cfs), ramping down 500 cfs/day according to the average classification. 

á       Base flows for June 2010 through May 23 2011 are calculated to reach the highest possible May 1, 2011 ULDE.
